# Story Bank — Master STAR+R Stories

This file accumulates your best interview stories over time. Each evaluation (Block F) adds new stories here. Instead of memorizing 100 answers, maintain 5-10 deep stories that you can bend to answer almost any behavioral question.

## How it works

1. Every time `/career-ops oferta` generates Block F (Interview Plan), new STAR+R stories get appended here
2. Before your next interview, review this file — your stories are already organized by theme
3. The "Big Three" questions can be answered with stories from this bank:
   - "Tell me about yourself" → combine 2-3 stories into a narrative
   - "Tell me about your most impactful project" → pick your highest-impact story
   - "Tell me about a conflict you resolved" → find a story with a Reflection

### [Systems / Process Improvement] Building a Planning System People Actually Trust
**Source:** Jeremy (Unique Appliances) — added 2026-05-03
**Company context:** Director, Supply Chain & Technology — 500+ SKUs, 4 distribution centers, manual spreadsheet-driven planning environment

**S (Situation):** The business was relying on manual, spreadsheet-driven planning with limited visibility into demand, inventory, lead times, and service risk across a large SKU base. Decisions were reactive and the numbers used in leadership discussions lacked credibility.

**T (Task):** Lead a more structured planning approach that would improve forecast quality, inventory control, and decision-making — without breaking existing operations in the process.

**A (Action):** Designed and implemented a system-led planning framework by translating business needs into Slim4 and NetSuite logic, defining key planning parameters, mapping data sources, and working through the workflow with supply chain, IT, and external consultants. Standardized reporting inputs so the planning process could shift from reactive spreadsheet reconciliation to exception-based decision-making.

**R (Result):** The organization gained a scalable planning process with statistical run rates built into forecasts as a point of comparison — moving from gut-feel estimates to data-anchored baselines. Review cadence shifted from bi-weekly to near real-time, and planning prep time dropped from 10 hours to under 2. Manual reconciliation was reduced, planning credibility improved across teams, and system adoption increased 85%.

**Reflection:** The most valuable analytics work is not just building reports — it is building a decision system that people trust and can actually use. Adoption follows trust, not training.

**Best for questions about:**
- "Tell me about a time you led a systems implementation"
- "How do you drive change in an organization resistant to new tools?"
- "Tell me about a process improvement you led"
- "How do you work with cross-functional teams and external consultants?"
- "Tell me about a time you improved data quality or reporting credibility"
- "What's your approach to ERP or planning system rollouts?"

### [Forecasting / Capacity Planning] Building Demand Models That Drive $800M in Decisions
**Source:** Jeremy (LifeScan / J&J) — added 2026-05-05
**Company context:** Global Supply Chain & Operations Leader — 12+ global markets, $800M USD diabetes device portfolio

**S (Situation):** LifeScan's global supply strategy needed to translate commercial demand signals into production plans across 12+ markets and multi-channel distribution — without a unified forecasting methodology to anchor decisions.

**T (Task):** Build and operate forecasting models that could support $800M annual production planning and inventory allocation decisions with enough credibility to drive executive-level AOP conversations.

**A (Action):** Developed leading-indicator consumption forecast models using patient/user base growth signals, channel mix data, and market-level trends integrated into SAP and JDA. Governed the monthly S&OP process, translating forecast outputs into executable production plans and inventory deployment decisions.

**R (Result):** Models underpinned $800M USD in annual supply planning decisions across 12+ global markets. Forecast accuracy improved to the point where leadership used the models as primary inputs into AOP, regional allocation, and inventory buffer decisions. System adoption increased 85% after integrating the planning methodology into ERP tooling.

**Reflection:** The credibility of a forecast isn't just in the math — it's in how the assumptions are documented and how the misses are explained. A model people trust is built over time through transparent variance review, not through claiming accuracy upfront.

**Best for questions about:**
- "Tell me about a time you built an analytical model from scratch"
- "How do you approach demand forecasting?"
- "Tell me about a time your analysis directly influenced a major business decision"
- "How do you build credibility with senior leadership through data?"
- "Describe your experience with capacity or workforce planning models"

### [Regulated Process Design] Designing a Compliant Return Workflow Under Regulatory Constraints (Project Ironside)
**Source:** Jeremy (LifeScan / J&J) — added 2026-05-05
**Company context:** Global Supply Chain & Operations Leader — medical device (blood glucose monitoring) reverse logistics for recalls and patient returns

**S (Situation):** LifeScan faced critical product safety risks from medical device recalls and returns. The existing reverse logistics process couldn't meet the lot-management, QA, and regulatory compliance requirements for a medical device manufacturer — creating legal, safety, and brand exposure.

**T (Task):** Lead a cross-functional transformation of the reverse logistics workflow that would ensure 100% regulatory compliance while reducing operational cost and improving return-to-inventory cycle time.

**A (Action):** Mapped the end-to-end return workflow against regulatory and QA requirements (lot-level traceability, compliance checkpoints, documentation standards). Evaluated and selected a specialized third-party 3PL with medical device handling expertise. Designed the new process, built compliance checkpoints into every handoff, and managed the transition with minimal disruption to active recall programs.

**R (Result):** Achieved 100% adherence to lot-management and regulatory compliance requirements post-transition. Reduced return processing costs by 15–20% and improved return-to-inventory cycle time by 20%. Mitigated critical product safety risk that had been flagged at the executive level.

**Reflection:** Regulated workflows have zero margin for "move fast and fix it later." The design work — stakeholder alignment, compliance mapping, 3PL vetting — had to be done before any process went live. Speed here was in the preparation, not the execution.

**Best for questions about:**
- "Tell me about a time you redesigned a complex process under regulatory constraints"
- "How do you manage risk in an operational transformation?"
- "Tell me about a time you selected and onboarded a vendor or partner"
- "Describe a situation where compliance and efficiency were in tension"
- "Tell me about a high-stakes project with significant operational risk"

### [Procurement / Vendor Management] Running a $350M Vendor Ordering Program at 99% Service Level
**Source:** Jeremy (LifeScan / J&J) — added 2026-05-06
**Company context:** Global Supply Chain & Operations Leader — CVS direct vendor ordering program, $350-400M USD annual business

**S (Situation):** CVS required near-perfect on-time and in-stock performance on a $350-400M annual vendor ordering program. Service level failure meant delistings, chargebacks, and commercial relationship risk at one of LifeScan's largest retail accounts.

**T (Task):** Manage the end-to-end vendor replenishment cycle — from forecast alignment to PO execution to OTIF tracking — treating 99% service level as the non-negotiable floor.

**A (Action):** Structured an ordering cadence tied to sell-through signals rather than push-based inventory targets. Built forecasting models aligned to retail consumption patterns, governed service level agreements with clear KPI transparency on both sides, and maintained direct communication with the CVS replenishment team to catch exceptions before they became failures.

**R (Result):** Sustained 99% on-time/in-stock performance across a $350-400M annual program. The KPI transparency structure kept both sides accountable and reduced escalation frequency over time.

**Reflection:** Service level agreements need teeth, not goodwill. The transparency — both sides seeing the same numbers — was what kept the relationship honest and eliminated the blame cycle when misses happened.

**Best for questions about:**
- "Tell me about a time you managed a high-stakes vendor relationship"
- "How do you manage procurement performance at scale?"
- "Tell me about a time you maintained service levels under pressure"
- "Describe your experience with retail replenishment or CPFR"
- "How do you structure vendor accountability?"

### [Sourcing / Negotiation] Diversifying a Supply Base to Neutralize Tariff Risk
**Source:** Jeremy (Unique Appliances) — added 2026-05-06
**Company context:** Director, Supply Chain & Technology — China-dependent supplier base, ~500 SKUs, appliances sector

**S (Situation):** Unique Appliances sourced almost exclusively from China, creating concentrated exposure to tariff volatility and geopolitical supply risk. The existing supplier base had no fallback if China-origin costs or access deteriorated further.

**T (Task):** Design and execute a multi-year sourcing strategy to diversify manufacturing geographically — reducing landed cost and insulating the business from trade risk — without disrupting current supply continuity.

**A (Action):** Ran a structured supplier qualification process across Turkey, Jordan, and Thailand — evaluating against quality, lead time, cost, and compliance criteria. Negotiated new supply contracts, ran GMROI analysis to rationalize the SKU portfolio being sourced, and executed a parallel migration to DDP incoterms to reduce import compliance overhead.

**R (Result):** Captured 15%+ in landed cost savings through the sourcing shift and an additional 7-10% from the DDP incoterm migration. Business was effectively insulated from the most acute geopolitical supply chain risks while maintaining supply continuity across 350+ containers annually.

**Reflection:** Should have built dual-sourcing requirements into new supplier contracts from the start — the initial contracts were single-source. The resilience came from the diversification, not the contracts themselves. Next time: write geographic diversity into the supply agreement, not as an add-on later.

**Best for questions about:**
- "Tell me about a time you reduced supply chain risk"
- "How do you approach supplier selection across new geographies?"
- "Tell me about a major cost reduction initiative you led"
- "Describe your experience with global sourcing and vendor negotiation"
- "How do you handle tariff or trade risk in your supply chain?"
